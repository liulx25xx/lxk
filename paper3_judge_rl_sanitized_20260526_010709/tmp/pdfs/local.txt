470 5.2 Position Confound as Causal Confounder
471 The training data contains three variables: query
472 content X, response quality Q (the intended sig-
473 nal), and response position P (the confound).
474 When P perfectly predicts the gold label, the
475 model has two reward paths: content-based (X →
476 Q → v ∗ ) and positional (P → v ∗ ). Because the
477 positional path is simpler and perfectly correlated,
478 it receives disproportionate reinforcement. Bal-
479 anced data removes the P → v ∗ edge, forcing the
480 model to rely on content. The accuracy difference
481 between unbalanced and balanced training quanti-
482 fies the causal contribution of the position path.
483 We validate this with a dose-response exper-
484 iment: training at intermediate confound ratios
485 (60%–95% position-A) produces proportionally
486 different shortcut severities, with an overall de-
487 cline in consistency as the confound approaches
488 the fully unbalanced setting (Figure 5).

5.4 Implications for Published Results 499
Our findings imply that reported accuracy 500
improvements from judge RL training likely 501
overstate genuine judgment capability. To em- 502
pirically verify this, we evaluated JudgeLRM’s 503
publicly released checkpoints (Chen et al., 2025) 504
with position-swap consistency testing (Table 5). 505
JudgeLRM-7B achieves 81.3% accuracy with 506
75.7% consistency. JudgeLRM-3B shows 76.8% 507
accuracy with 67.9% consistency. In both cases, 508
the A-selection rate equals accuracy, meaning 509
correct predictions rely on first-position prefer- 510
ence. The severity gradient reappears: the smaller 511
model’s consistency gap is larger. Position-swap 512
consistency testing is necessary to separate 513
genuine capability from positional artifact. 514
